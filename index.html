🔬 STELLAR ANATOMY — INTERACTIVE CROSS-SECTION

ATMOSPHERE & OUTER CRUST

A thin plasma of H/He/C. The crust is a crystalline lattice of neutron-rich nuclei — the strongest solid known, 10¹⁰× stronger than steel by density ratio. Superfluid neutrons flow freely here. Pulsar glitches originate from vortex unpinning in this layer.

OUTER CORE

Neutron fluid with ~5% proton/electron admixture. Protons form a Type II superconductor; neutrons a superfluid — both quantum phases. Responsible for most of the star's mass and directly probed by LIGO tidal deformability measurements.

INNER CORE — TERRA INCOGNITA

The most unknown region in all of physics. May contain hyperons (strange baryons), deconfined quark-gluon plasma, kaon condensates, or hybrid matter. Above 5ρ₀ all standard nuclear models break down. Einstein Telescope may finally see this.

DARK MATTER ADMIXTURE?

New 2025 research proposes captured dark matter accumulates at the core, forming a DM admixture changing the mass-radius relation. DM fractions as low as 1% are detectable by NICER. For asymmetric DM without annihilation — the accumulated mass could eventually collapse to a micro-black hole, destroying the star from inside.

01
Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff Equation
dP/dr = −(ε+P)(m+4πr³P/c²) / [r²(1−2Gm/rc²)]

The TOV equation is GR's equivalent of hydrostatic equilibrium for dense stars. It describes how pressure P must vary with radius r to prevent gravitational collapse. Unlike Newtonian gravity, it includes pressure itself as a source of gravity (the P terms in the numerator) — which paradoxically means squeezing a neutron star harder increases the gravity trying to collapse it. There exists a maximum mass beyond which no solution exists and the star must become a black hole. This maximum mass directly constrains the EOS — a stiffer EOS (higher pressure at given density) supports a higher mass limit.

02
Gravitational Redshift
z = (1 − 2GM/Rc²)^(−½) − 1

Light climbing out of a deep gravitational well loses energy — its wavelength stretches (redshifts). For a neutron star with compactness 2GM/Rc² ≈ 0.4, the surface redshift z ≈ 0.3 — meaning an atom emitting photons at 1000 nm on the surface is observed at 1300 nm from infinity. This is directly measurable in X-ray spectroscopy and provides a simultaneous mass-radius constraint. For a black hole, the formula diverges (z→∞) at the Schwarzschild radius — light cannot escape. Neutron stars with z>0.5 are hypothetically possible with extreme EOS.

03
Pulsar Magnetic Field Estimate
B ≈ 3.2×10¹⁹ √(P Ṗ) Gauss

Pulsars spin down because rotating magnetic dipoles radiate energy. Assuming all spin-down energy goes into magnetic dipole radiation, the surface field is estimated from P (period in seconds) and Ṗ (period derivative, dimensionless but ×10⁻¹⁵ typically). Millisecond pulsars have P~0.003 s, Ṗ~10⁻²⁰ → B~10⁸ G. Young pulsars have P~0.1 s, Ṗ~10⁻¹⁵ → B~10¹² G. Magnetars have Ṗ~10⁻¹¹ → B~10¹⁵ G. The P–Ṗ diagram plotting all known pulsars reveals their entire evolutionary history.

04
Tidal Deformability (GW EOS Probe)
Λ = (2/3) k₂ (R/M)⁵  ·  GW phase: ψ(f) ∝ −(39/2) Λ̃ (πMf)^(−10/3)

When two neutron stars spiral together, each tidally distorts the other's shape. This deformation requires energy, modifying the gravitational waveform phase ψ(f) measurable by LIGO. The tidal deformability Λ depends sensitively on R⁵ — a stiff EOS (large R) produces large Λ, easily spotted in GW data. GW170817 measured Λ₁.₄ < 800, ruling out R > ~13 km and eliminating many EOS models. Future detectors (Einstein Telescope) will measure Λ to ~1% — essentially pinning down the EOS across the neutron star mass range.

05
Dark Matter Capture Rate
Γ_cap ∝ (ρ_DM / m_χ) × σ_χN × (GM/R) × N_n

The capture rate of dark matter by a neutron star depends on: the local DM density ρ_DM (highest in galactic center), the DM mass m_χ (lower mass = more particles per unit density), the DM-nucleon scattering cross-section σ_χN (what direct detection experiments measure), the gravitational focusing factor GM/R (large for NS), and the number of target nucleons N_n. Old neutron stars near the galactic center accumulate ~10³⁴–10⁴⁰ DM particles over 10 Gyr — enough to heat, modify the EOS, or destabilize the star. This formula is the bridge between fundamental particle physics and astrophysical observation.

💬 FREQUENTLY ASKED
Could a neutron star destroy Earth if it passed nearby?+

Yes — but the probability is negligible. Within ~100 AU, gravitational disruption would destabilize planetary orbits. Within ~1000 km, the magnetar-class field would strip Earth's atmosphere via extreme X-ray/gamma radiation. The nearest known neutron star (RX J1856) is ~400 light-years away and moving away from us. In 4.5 billion years of solar system history, no neutron star has come remotely close to disturbing our orbit.

Why don't neutron stars collapse into black holes?+

Neutron stars are supported by neutron degeneracy pressure — a quantum mechanical consequence of the Pauli exclusion principle, which forbids two neutrons from occupying the same quantum state. When compressed together, neutrons resist further compression with immense pressure independent of temperature. Unlike thermal pressure, this cannot be "cooled away." However, above the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit (~2–3 M☉), gravity overwhelms even neutron degeneracy pressure and collapse to a black hole is unavoidable.

How do neutron stars spin 700 times per second?+

Conservation of angular momentum — the ice skater effect. A progenitor star rotating once per month collapses its core from ~10,000 km to ~10 km, reducing the moment of inertia by a factor of ~10⁹. Since L = Iω is conserved, the spin rate increases by 10⁹ — turning a monthly rotation into milliseconds. Millisecond pulsars spin even faster because they've been recycled: accreting matter from a companion star adds angular momentum, spinning them up to 716 Hz (PSR J1748-2446ad).

What is the gold in my ring actually made from?+

Neutron star mergers. The r-process (rapid neutron capture) requires neutron fluxes only available in NS mergers and some core-collapse supernovae. When two neutron stars collide, the neutron-rich ejecta (~0.01–0.05 M☉) undergoes explosive r-process synthesis, building nuclei up to uranium in ~1 second. The 2017 kilonova AT2017gfo directly observed this, showing spectra matching strontium, lanthanides, and very likely gold. Each merger produces ~5–10 Earth masses of gold. The gold atoms in your ring were forged in a neutron star collision roughly 5–10 billion years before the solar system formed.

Is there actually something solid inside a neutron star?+

Yes — the outer crust (~1 km thick) is a genuine solid: a Coulomb crystal lattice of neutron-rich nuclei (primarily ⁶²Fe, ⁶⁴Ni) in a sea of relativistic electrons. By shear modulus-to-density ratio, it is ~10¹⁰ times stronger than steel — the strongest solid known. But "mountains" on this surface are limited to ~5 mm height by the crushing gravity. Below the crust is the inner crust with superfluid neutrons filling the space between nuclei, then the core — a liquid sea of quantum fluids, not a solid in any conventional sense.

What exactly is a "glitch" in pulsar timing?+

A pulsar glitch is a sudden, unexpected spin-up — the pulsar abruptly rotates faster, then slowly relaxes back. The leading explanation involves the inner crust's superfluid neutrons: neutron vortices (quantized rotation lines in the superfluid) become pinned to the nuclear lattice. As the outer crust spins down over years, angular momentum builds up in the pinned vortex array. Eventually the vortices unpin catastrophically — transferring angular momentum from the superfluid to the crust in milliseconds, spinning it up. The relaxation timescale after a glitch constrains the superfluid moment of inertia and thus the crust thickness — a direct probe of the EOS.
